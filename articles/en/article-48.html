2. What Problems Does Space Computing Solve?

To understand why space computing has suddenly become an industry focal point, we need to understand three structural bottlenecks of ground-based computing:

First, coverage blind spots. Approximately 3 billion people worldwide remain in areas with weak network coverage. Oceans, deserts, polar regions, and high-altitude scenarios are beyond the reach of ground networks. For deep-sea shipping, emergency rescue, field exploration, and aerospace scenarios, ground computing has natural coverage limitations.

Second, transmission latency. The physical distance between ground data centers and terminals sets an upper limit on latency. Even with optimal fiber optic networks, transoceanic transmission latency exceeds 100ms. For autonomous driving, real-time remote sensing, and industrial control, this latency is unacceptable. In-orbit computing can process data locally, reducing latency from hundreds of milliseconds to microseconds.

Third, energy and land bottlenecks. Ground data centers are energy hogs—global data center electricity consumption accounts for 2-3% of total global electricity. In China, land and power resources in eastern coastal regions are increasingly strained, with some cities imposing restrictions on new data center construction. Space computing can utilize solar energy, achieving zero carbon emissions without land constraints.

The core value of space computing can be summarized in one sentence: Deploy computing power where data is generated, rather than transmitting data to where computing power exists.

For remote sensing satellites, petabytes of data are generated daily, but limited by satellite-to-ground transmission bandwidth, vast amounts of data cannot be downloaded. If satellites themselves possess powerful in-orbit computing capabilities, they can complete data filtering, compression, and inference in orbit, transmitting only the most valuable results back to Earth. This is not just efficiency improvement—it is a paradigm shift from "transmit data back to compute" to "compute where data is generated."

The SenseTime-Guoxing Aerospace partnership model differs fundamentally from the US approach. 4. Technical Breakdown of Shanghai's Star Pivot Plan

As Shanghai's flagship space computing constellation project, the Star Pivot Plan's debut constellation features a "one master, two auxiliary" satellite cluster architecture, forming an independent closed-loop minimum computing validation unit:

Central Computing Master Satellite—"Space Brain"

The master satellite serves as the global scheduling core and computing hub, featuring a self-developed distributed hybrid computing scheduling platform. Its technical highlights include:

  • Domestic aerospace-grade GPU chip: 32 PFLOPS in-orbit compute, filling China's gap in large-scale satellite in-situ real-time computing
  • Embodied intelligent O&M system: First embodied intelligence system on a Chinese computing satellite, enabling in-orbit GPU module reconfiguration
  • Three-tier thermal management: Aerospace-grade microchannel active liquid cooling + multi-layer ultra-light passive insulation + large deployable radiator, China's first long-term stable thermal solution for in-orbit GPUs
  • 400Gbps inter-satellite laser communication: Breakthrough in high-speed laser communication for computing interconnect
  • Perovskite-silicon tandem solar cells: 15KW power generation, first satellite use of this technology

Together, the three satellites form a complete space-based intelligent service chain—from computing scheduling to weather sensing to remote sensing imaging—all data processed in orbit without downloading massive raw data.

5. Space Computing Industry Chain Panorama

Space computing is not a single track but a complete industry chain spanning upstream chips, midstream satellite manufacturing and operations, and downstream applications:

Upstream: Onboard AI Chips and Core Components

This is the highest-barrier segment. The space environment imposes stringent requirements—radiation resistance, wide temperature range, high reliability. Key domestic players include: AeroMicro (Yulong series aerospace-grade AI chips, the only full-chain closed-loop solution), Cambricon (MLU370-X8, 744 TOPS, supporting 10B-parameter models in orbit), and Fudan Microelectronics (aerospace-grade anti-radiation FPGAs, >80% market share).

Midstream: Satellite Manufacturing and Constellation Operations

Includes satellite manufacturing, constellation operations, and ground control. Guoxing Aerospace has experience launching nearly 100 commercial satellites—a key asset in the SenseTime partnership.

Downstream: Applications and Data Services

Space computing applications are rapidly expanding: weather forecasting, emergency response, ocean shipping, financial analysis, and defense. These scenarios share common characteristics: large data volumes, high timeliness requirements, and insufficient ground network coverage.

6. Relationship with Ground AI Infrastructure

The rise of space computing does not mean the end of ground computing. On the contrary, the two will form a complementary "space-earth integrated" computing system.

Ground computing handles large-scale training, complex inference, and cold data storage. Space computing handles edge inference, real-time processing, and global coverage. SenseTime Co-founder Yang Fan described it aptly: "For emergency rescue, ocean vessels, and field operations in weak network environments, space computing can effectively fill the coverage blind spots of ground networks. Its value extends beyond capability boundary breakthroughs—it is infrastructure supporting China's AI reach to the global stage."

From an industry evolution perspective, AI infrastructure is undergoing a triple jump: from single-point computing to system computing to space-earth integrated computing.

Notably, this contrasts sharply with the "Meta transforming into a computing seller" story (article-35). Meta does ground computing "outsourcing"—leasing idle GPU capacity. The SenseTime Constellation does space computing "native"—building entirely new computing infrastructure in space. The former is redistribution of existing resources; the latter is creation of incremental space.

Risk Warnings

Space computing remains in early stages with the following risks:

  • Technical validation risk: Long-term reliability in deep space, real thermal performance in microgravity, energy subsystem cycle efficiency—all require in-orbit validation
  • Launch cost risk: Despite declining commercial launch costs, large-scale constellation deployment requires massive capital investment
  • Orbital resource competition: Low Earth orbit resources are increasingly scarce
  • Geopolitical risk: Space computing involves national security, export controls and international cooperation restrictions may impact development
  • Commercialization cycle risk: From prototype to commercial scale typically takes 5-10 years; short-term performance delivery is challenging
