1. Valuation Restructuring Behind the Trillion-Dollar Market Cap

On June 13, 2026, SpaceX made its highly anticipated stock market debut. IPO shares surged 19% on debut, closing at $161, giving the company a market cap of approximately $1.95 trillion, making it one of the highest-valued private enterprises globally, surpassing Tesla.

This valuation perplexes traditional investors: SpaceX's core business — rocket launch services — generates only about $9 billion in annual revenue. Using traditional valuation logic, even giving the space launch business a 50x P/E ratio, market cap would barely exceed $500 billion. So where does the extra $1.4 trillion+ valuation premium come from?

The answer lies in an investor briefing leaked before the IPO. The document clearly states: "SpaceX is not a rocket launch company but an AI-era computing infrastructure company." This sentence redefines the capital market's valuation framework.

Forbes immediately updated its rich list: Elon Musk, with approximately $1.02 trillion in personal net worth, officially became humanity's first "trillionaire." SpaceX shares contributed over $600 billion to this wealth creation.

2. Trinity: SpaceX's AI Infrastructure Layout

The investor briefing reveals SpaceX's strategic core logic: building an AI-era "Trinity" infrastructure system.

First Pillar: Starlink Satellite Network

As of June 2026, Starlink's in-orbit satellite count exceeds 7,000, covering over 98% of the global population. More importantly, these satellites are upgrading from "communication pipes" to "compute carriers."

The new-generation Starlink satellites SpaceX is testing each carry up to 10 PetaFLOPS of edge computing power. This means the entire Starlink network's theoretical compute can reach 70 ExaFLOPS — equivalent to the combined compute of dozens of hyperscale data centers.

Second Pillar: Starbase Cloud Platform

SpaceX's secretly developed two-year "Starbase Cloud" is finally emerging. This is a distributed cloud computing platform based on the Starlink satellite network, with core selling points: global coverage, unlimited scaling, pay-per-use.

Unlike traditional cloud service providers, Starbase Cloud doesn't require ground data centers, inherently possessing disaster resilience and high availability. First test customers include multiple AI startups with "use-as-needed, globally accessible" compute demands.

Third Pillar: Starship Energy Infrastructure

This is SpaceX's most aggressive strategic initiative. Starship's ultimate goal isn't just a launch vehicle but becoming a "space power station." SpaceX's vision: establishing nuclear power plants on the Moon and Mars, transmitting clean energy back to Earth.

This plan sounds like science fiction, but SpaceX has signed a letter of intent with the US Department of Energy to research space-based solar power transmission technology. In the short term, Starship will become the world's largest chemical propellant producer, providing backup power solutions for data centers.

3. $920 Million Monthly: Google and SpaceX's Strategic Deal

Just before SpaceX's IPO, a regulatory filing disclosed Google-SpaceX cloud computing collaboration details, instantly igniting the capital market.

According to the filing, Google will pay SpaceX approximately $920 million monthly to rent Starlink network computing and communication services for at least 36 months. The total deal value exceeds $33 billion.

This is a deal that changes the industry landscape:

  • For Google: Gained a globally edge-covered compute network, providing Gemini AI with lower latency and higher availability inference services.
  • For SpaceX: Secured a strategic customer generating over $10 billion annually, providing solid performance support for IPO valuation.
  • For the industry: Space infrastructure officially enters the AI industry chain; valuation logic needs complete restructuring.

Notably, this isn't simply "bandwidth rental" but deep technical integration: Google's TPU chips will be deployed on Starlink satellites, and SpaceX's edge computing nodes will run Google's AI models. This is an "Space AI" prototype.

4. Anthropic's 1GW Craving: The Endless Gap in AI Compute

Just as Google and SpaceX reached an agreement, another news broke from the AI industry: Anthropic is seeking data center leases with capacity reaching 1GW (gigawatt).

What does 1GW represent? This is twice the capacity of currently the world's largest single data center campus (Microsoft's Phoenix data center in the US). If Anthropic's lease materializes, its electricity consumption will exceed some mid-sized cities.

This demand reveals the true state of the AI compute market: demand growth far exceeds supply expansion.

  • GPT-5's training consumed approximately 500MW of electricity.
  • Claude 4's inference services require continuous approximately 300MW power supply.
  • Next-generation model training + inference demands may exceed 2GW.

Traditional ground data center expansion speed can't keep up with AI demand growth. Land, power, and cooling water constraints make large data center site selection increasingly difficult. Space infrastructure — whether Starlink's edge compute or lunar power stations — represents the possibility of breaking through this bottleneck.

SpaceX keenly captured this trend. Starbase Cloud's positioning: "When ground compute falls short, space compute fills the gap."

5. KKR and NVIDIA: Capital Bets on AI Infrastructure

SpaceX isn't the only AI infrastructure company being repriced. The same week, KKR and NVIDIA jointly announced forming an AI infrastructure company with initial capital scale reaching $10 billion.

This investment logic aligns with SpaceX's valuation restructuring: AI-era "water, electricity, and gas" suppliers will become the most valuable assets.

KKR's choices reveal several important signals:

  • AI infrastructure is a long-term track: KKR, known for "patient capital," entering signals AI infrastructure investment is no longer pure tech speculation but has entered the "mature asset" stage.
  • NVIDIA's role upgraded: From chip supplier to infrastructure investor, NVIDIA is building vertical integration moats from chips to cloud services.
  • Diversified infrastructure landscape: SpaceX (space), KKR-NVIDIA (ground), Anthropic (self-built) — three parallel paths, AI infrastructure competition unfolding multi-dimensionally.

SpaceX CEO Musk stated in a post-IPO interview: "Looking back from ten years from now, today's valuation will seem very cheap. AI's demand for compute is unlimited, and companies that can provide unlimited compute have unlimited value."

6. Redefining Boundaries: The Compute Race from Earth to Space

SpaceX's IPO marks AI infrastructure competition entering a new phase: expanding from "ground data centers" to "space edge computing."

This transformation brings three fundamental changes:

First, geographic boundaries disappear. Traditional data centers are constrained by land, power, and network infrastructure, with limited choices. Starlink deploys compute nodes into orbit, theoretically providing AI services to any corner of Earth.

Second, cost structure reshapes. Space infrastructure's initial investment is huge, but marginal cost approaches zero. When Starlink's 7,000 satellites complete deployment, expanding compute will cost far less than ground data center expansion.

Third, strategic value surges. Owning space compute infrastructure means possessing "AI service capabilities not dependent on ground networks." In disasters, wars, or geopolitical conflicts, this will be an irreplaceable strategic asset.

Of course, Space AI infrastructure faces challenges:

  • Satellite lifespan is limited (typically 5-7 years), requiring continuous replacement.
  • Space radiation's impact on chip stability not yet fully solved.
  • International telecommunications regulations' disputes over orbital resource allocation.

But these challenges haven't dampened capital enthusiasm whatsoever. SpaceX IPO's oversubscription ratio reached an astonishing 47x, setting a historical record for private enterprise IPOs.

Conclusion

SpaceX's $1.95 trillion market cap is a footnote of our era: when AI becomes the new electricity, its infrastructure — whether ground data centers, space satellite networks, or future lunar power stations — will all be repriced.

Elon Musk becoming the first trillionaire isn't just one person's wealth mythology but a signal: AI-era wealth logic is migrating from the "application layer" to the "infrastructure layer".

Google's $920 million monthly rent, Anthropic's 1GW craving, KKR and NVIDIA's billion-dollar bets — these numbers collectively point to one conclusion: AI infrastructure is the most certain investment track of the next decade.

SpaceX's IPO may just be the beginning. When a "rocket company" can be valued at $1.95 trillion, AI infrastructure's imagination is just opening.
