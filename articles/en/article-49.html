WAIC 2026 Full Panorama — AI Enters the 'Year of Delivery': From Parameter Competition to Value Delivery, Four Defining Trends

From July 17 to 20, 2026, WAIC 2026 was held in Shanghai under the theme 'Intelligent Partners, Co-creating the Future.' With 1,100+ exhibitors, 300+ global premieres, and an exhibition area exceeding 100,000 square meters for the first time, the event marked a pivotal inflection point — as the noise of parameter competition fades, the AI industry is transitioning from 'proving what it can do' to 'actually doing what you need.'

On July 20, 2026, the Shanghai World Expo Center hosted the closing of WAIC 2026. Over four days, 1,100 enterprises exhibited, more than 300 products made their global debut, and the exhibition area surpassed 100,000 square meters for the first time. These numbers tell a story: AI is no longer a laboratory concept — it is becoming an 'infrastructure' permeating every corner of industry.

The theme of WAIC 2026, 'Intelligent Partners, Co-creating the Future,' reflects a fundamental shift in the industry's orientation: from chasing parameters and benchmarking training scale to focusing on products, user experience, and tangible delivery. This article unpacks the four major trends revealed at WAIC 2026, supplemented by the 'Top Ten Treasures of the Hall,' exhibitor dynamics, and investment signals, to present a panoramic view of AI's 'Year of Delivery.'

1. Embodied AI: From 'Dancing' to 'Working'

WAIC 2026 exhibition area, Zhiyuan A3 Ultra humanoid robot performing live operational tasks

If one track stood out above all others at WAIC 2026, it was embodied AI. More than 200 enterprises exhibited, with over 300 robots operating dynamically on-site — nearly triple the number from last year. Importantly, these robots were no longer 'dancing' or 'doing backflips' for show; they were demonstrating real work capabilities.

Zhiyuan's A3 Ultra was among the 'Top Ten Treasures of the Hall' and exemplified the transition from concept to mass production. Zhiyuan Robotics announced that cumulative A3 Ultra shipments have exceeded 15,000 units, deployed across industrial manufacturing, logistics sorting, and commercial service scenarios. At the exhibition, the A3 Ultra demonstrated precision component assembly and flexible material handling — actions that may appear less dramatic than 'somersaults' but signify a fundamental leap from 'watchable' to 'usable.'

Ant Lingbo Robot Smart Pharmacy marked another milestone. The system has already entered operations at Guoda Pharmacy, automating the entire process from drug storage and sorting to dispensing. This proves a critical point: embodied AI commercialization need not start with the hardest general-purpose scenarios; solving specific problems in vertical niches can yield faster product-market fit.

WAIC 2026 also featured a dedicated 'Embodied AI Application Zone' where visitors could operate robots to perform daily tasks such as pouring water, folding clothes, and retrieving objects. This shift from 'watching' to 'using' is itself a sign of industry maturation.

However, sober voices were also heard. Multiple industry insiders noted that embodied AI remains in the Proof-of-Concept stage, with significant distance from large-scale commercialization. Core bottlenecks include insufficient general manipulation capabilities, high per-unit costs, and the need for extensive customization. One partner at a leading investment firm remarked: 'Embodied AI's biggest challenge isn't technology — it's finding enough paying scenarios.'

2. The Year of the Agent Phone: From 'Voice Assistant' to 'Proactive Intelligence'

If embodied AI was the physical highlight of WAIC 2026, agent phones were the digital counterpart. The industry has dubbed 2026 the 'Year of the Agent Phone,' and WAIC 2026 was the definitive showcase.

StepFun's Step X Neo received WAIC's L3 Agent Certification — the first mobile-side model in China to achieve this level. The Step X Neo is capable of cross-app task orchestration, contextual memory, and proactive service recommendations. In one demonstration, a user simply said 'Plan a two-day weekend trip to Shanghai,' and the Step X Neo automatically invoked mapping, restaurant reviews, weather, and calendar apps to generate a complete itinerary synced to the calendar.

ByteDance's Doubao Phone Gen 2 NaviX Ultra made its public debut at WAIC. Featuring an upgraded Doubao model, the NaviX Ultra pioneers 'gaze interaction' — users simply look at information on the screen, and the phone automatically identifies it and displays relevant action menus. This interaction paradigm shift is considered a major breakthrough over traditional touch-based interfaces.

Honor's Robot Phone took an even bolder approach — it features a micro-robotic arm that can actively extend to help users pick up small objects on a desk or automatically align with a wireless charging coil. Although this design sparked debate about 'utility vs. gimmick,' Honor's message was clear: phone makers' understanding of 'agent' is extending from the software layer to the hardware layer.

However, agent phones face a significant homogenization problem. Walking the exhibition floor, virtually every phone maker was pitching 'agent assistants,' 'cross-app orchestration,' and 'proactive services' — descriptions so similar that users struggle to perceive substantive differences. A smartphone industry analyst noted: 'The biggest challenge for agent phones is scenario definition. Everyone can do it, but nobody has figured out which scenarios users truly need this capability.'

3. China's ExaFLOPS Computing Era: From 'Do We Have It' to 'How to Use It Well'

Sugon 8000 (Dengfeng) 100,000-card supercomputing cluster, marking China's entry into the ExaFLOPS computing era

Computing infrastructure was another major highlight of WAIC 2026. If last year's discussion was about 'whether we have enough computing power,' this year's conversation shifted to 'how to use it effectively.'

Sugon 8000 (Dengfeng) 100K-Card Ultra-Cluster was the most 'hardcore' of the Top Ten Treasures. It achieved large-scale deployment of 100,000 domestic AI chips in a single cluster, reaching international advanced levels in training efficiency, interconnect bandwidth, and energy efficiency. This marks a substantive step forward in China's self-sufficiency in ultra-large-scale computing clusters.

Huawei's Atlas 950 Supernode also debuted at WAIC. Featuring a new chip-to-chip interconnect architecture, the Atlas 950 delivers 3x the per-node compute power of its predecessor while reducing power consumption by 30%. Huawei built a live liquid-cooled server cluster display, giving visitors a tangible sense of domestic computing power.

At the '100,000-card' scale, China's AI computing is undergoing a leap from 'quantitative' to 'qualitative' change. Clusters of this size make previously 'impossible' large-scale distributed training tasks feasible. But challenges remain: cluster stability, training efficiency, and operational complexity are all new problems at this scale. A technical lead at a cloud computing provider noted: 'The engineering complexity of a 100,000-card cluster rises exponentially — it's not just about stacking more cards.'

Notably, WAIC showcased the resilience of China's domestic computing supply chain. Multiple domestic chip makers exhibited specialized AI inference chips whose energy efficiency approaches or even partially surpasses international counterparts. While high-end training chips still lag behind, the pace of domestic substitution in the inference segment is accelerating.

4. Token as the Unit of AI Economy: Operators Shift from Traffic to Token Management

One seemingly 'technical detail' at WAIC 2026 actually carries the deepest industrial significance: Token is becoming the core unit of measurement in the AI economy.

China's three major telecom operators all announced AI computing network strategies at WAIC, with a remarkably consistent direction — shifting from traffic management to token management. China Mobile launched an 'AI Computing Package' priced in Tokens, allowing users to transfer Tokens freely across cloud, edge nodes, and terminals. China Telecom introduced 'Tianyi Token Connect,' supporting cross-platform Token settlement. China Unicom released the 'Unicom Token Chain,' leveraging blockchain technology for transparent Token circulation and settlement.

This shift is as significant as the telecom industry's earlier transition from 'voice services' to 'data services.' Token as the fundamental unit of the AI economy is reshaping value distribution across the entire industry chain:

  • For model providers: Token pricing directly determines business models. Kimi K3's inference cost is just 1/4 of Claude Fable 5's — such pricing strategies are forcing the industry to rethink 'how much AI capabilities are worth.'
  • For cloud service providers: Token management means transitioning from 'selling resources' to 'selling capabilities.' Users no longer care how many GPUs are running underneath; they only care how many Tokens are needed to complete a task.
  • For enterprise users: Tokenization makes AI costs measurable, predictable, and optimizable. Enterprises can manage AI capability consumption the same way they manage cloud resources.

5. Sober Footnotes: Four Truths WAIC 2026 Revealed

Beneath WAIC 2026's spectacle, rational voices deserve equal attention. These four 'sober footnotes' form the other side of the story:

Footnote 1: Embodied AI is still in the POC stage. Despite 300+ robots dynamically operating, most exhibitors' robots were performing pre-programmed scenarios rather than operating autonomously in open environments. The 'last mile' from POC to commercial deployment may be the hardest ten miles.

Footnote 2: Terminal agents suffer from severe homogenization. Agent phone feature descriptions are strikingly similar across vendors. 'Cross-app orchestration' and 'proactive services' have become standard talking points. When everyone is doing the same thing, differentiation becomes the biggest challenge.

Footnote 3: Winner-take-all dynamics are intensifying. While exhibitor numbers hit a record high, only the top 20-30 companies had real 'presence.' Small and mid-sized startups increasingly struggle to gain attention at such events — resources and attention are concentrating at the top.

Footnote 4: Safety and compliance remain works in progress. Multiple forums addressed AI safety and governance, but implementation paths remain vague. From data privacy to model security, from algorithmic transparency to liability assignment, AI governance frameworks are still lagging behind technological development.

6. Investment Signals: Mutual Funds Swarm WAIC as AI Investing Enters Precision Research Phase

Multiple mutual funds dispatched research teams to conduct on-the-ground due diligence at WAIC 2026

WAIC 2026 welcomed a special group of 'visitors' — research teams from more than 30 mutual fund companies. According to the organizer, these teams conducted field research across AI chips, large models, embodied AI, and AI applications throughout the four-day event.

This signal indicates that AI investing is transitioning from 'sector betting' to 'precision research.' Over the past two years, AI investing was largely about 'buying the sector' — any company associated with AI could command a premium. But in 2026, capital is becoming more discerning: investors are no longer satisfied with 'we do AI'; they now ask 'what problem does your AI solve,' 'what's your gross margin,' and 'what's your customer retention rate.'

One fund manager participating in the research commented: 'The biggest takeaway from WAIC is that the AI industry is shifting from "telling stories" to "doing the math." In the past, we talked about how big the market is and how impressive the technology is. Now investors care more about whether you can make money and when.'

This assessment forms an interesting contrast with OpenAI's latest funding round. On July 20, OpenAI announced a $40 billion funding round led by SoftBank, valuing the company at $300 billion, with ChatGPT surpassing 500 million active users. On one hand, leading players continue to 'burn cash for scale,' while on the other, capital demands increasingly higher ROI from niche segments. This 'fire and ice' dynamic is defining AI investing in 2026.

7. Conclusion: The 'Year of Delivery' — AI's 'iPhone Moment' Hasn't Arrived, but the 'App Store Moment' Is Here

Looking back at WAIC 2026, a clear narrative emerges: the AI industry is transitioning from parameter competition to value delivery.

In 2024, the industry compared who had larger model parameters. In 2025, the focus was on who could deploy first. By 2026, the industry has entered the 'Year of Delivery' — not just deploying, but delivering well, fast, and cheaply.

The Sugon 8000's 100,000-card cluster, Zhiyuan's 15,000-unit A3 Ultra delivery, Ant Lingbo's deployed pharmacy, and the three operators' Token strategies — these are not 'news' items; they are 'deliveries.' They signify AI transitioning from 'proof-of-concept' to 'production-grade.'

WAIC 2026's theme was 'Intelligent Partners, Co-creating the Future.' The word 'co-creating' captures precisely the attitude the AI industry needs most right now — not AI replacing humans, but AI collaborating with humans to create value together. From parameter competition to value delivery, AI is moving from 'I am here' to 'I can help.'

AI's 'iPhone Moment' may not have arrived yet, but the 'App Store Moment' is certainly here. When infrastructure is ready, development tools are mature, and business models are clear, the real application explosion is just around the corner.
