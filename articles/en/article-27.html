China's AI Governance Framework Takes Shape: 40+ National Standards plus 7 Agent Interconnection Standards, Full-Stack Regulation from Compute to Agent Collaboration

On 2026-06-26, China's National Standardization Administration unveiled 40+ AI standards and 7 Agent Interconnection standards simultaneously, marking the industry watershed from "unbridled growth" to "regulated deep cultivation"

On June 26, 2026, China's National Standardization Administration unveiled two landmark achievements simultaneously — over 40 national AI standards completed and published since 2025, covering foundational support, key technologies and product applications; alongside the official release of 7 national standards in the "Artificial Intelligence — Agent Interconnection" series. From AI server performance benchmarks, to large model R&D specifications, to agent identity codes, collaborative interactions and tool-calling protocols — the full-stack contours of China's AI governance framework are revealed to the world for the first time.

This is not an ordinary policy release, but a systemic supply of rules. It signals that China's AI industry is shifting from "compute races" and "model wars" toward "regulated, scalable industrial deployment." This article unpacks four dimensions: the full-stack meaning of the 40 standards, the deep logic of the 7 agent interconnection standards, the global discourse contest, and the long-term impact on China's AI value chain.

I. Full-Stack Meaning of the 40 Standards: A Rule Loop from Compute to Endpoint

The cost of a decade of unbridled AI growth is plain to see — ten different interface protocols for the same type of product, twenty different test methods for the same capability, and "pseudo-AI products" running rampant. The core value of national standards is not to constrain innovation, but to establish comparable, traceable and trustworthy industrial baselines.

The 40+ standards span three blocks, forming a complete industry rule stack:

  • Foundation Layer: AI server performance test methods, AI compute center construction specs, dataset quality assessment
  • Key Technology Layer: Large model R&D specs, AI maturity assessment, world model and ontology model technical requirements, trustworthy AI evaluation
  • Product Application Layer: Terminal intelligence grading, high-value scenario evaluation, industry AI solution interfaces

The most consequential clause is the explicit goal of "identifying pseudo-AI products."

Once national standards define what qualifies as an AI product, vendors riding the AI hype with sticker labels will be rapidly weeded out, while companies with genuine core technology will reap regulatory dividends. This explains why A-share AI application stocks rallied right after the June 26 announcement — the market read the signal: standardization equals concentration toward leaders.

II. Agent Interconnection Standards: Filling the Most Critical Gap of "AI Phase Two"

If the 40 standards address the "industrial foundation" problem, then the 7 Agent Interconnection standards address what we might call the "collaboration problem of AI Phase Two".

The 7 standards cover the entire agent lifecycle:

  1. Overall Architecture: Defines the master framework and reference model for agent interconnection
  2. Identity Code: Issues a "digital ID" to every agent
  3. Identity Management: Authentication, authorization, trust endorsement
  4. Agent Description: Unified description of capability boundaries and service interfaces
  5. Agent Discovery: Cross-platform, cross-vendor registration and lookup
  6. Agent Interaction: Communication protocols and session semantics between agents
  7. Agent Tool Calling: Standardized interface for calling external tools and APIs

It looks technical, but the industrial implications are profound. Every Chinese tech giant today — ByteDance, Alibaba, Tencent, Baidu, Zhipu, Moonshot, DeepSeek — is building its own agent platform, each with its own protocol. An enterprise client wanting to orchestrate agents across 4 vendors needs 6 sets of adapters.

This is the "information silo" the national standard solves. Unified identity, unified description, unified protocol — equivalent to issuing all agents a common "language textbook". The State Administration for Market Regulation has explicitly indicated that the identity code standard will be upgraded to a mandatory national standard in due course.

Global Context: China Leads on Agent Collaboration

Globally, no country or international body has yet released a complete, systemic agent interconnection standard. Anthropic's MCP (Model Context Protocol) and OpenAI's Function Calling remain at single-vendor product-level protocol layer. China's leadership in establishing this at the national standard level means — whoever sets the rules first holds the high ground in the discourse battle of AI Phase Two.

III. Industrial Impact: A Three-Tier Beneficiary Map

Who actually benefits once the policy lands? This is what industrial capital cares about most. We map the beneficiaries into three tiers:

Tier 1: Platform Champions Who Co-Authored the Standards

Companies that participated directly in drafting the national standards have technology roadmaps best aligned with the spec, and will reap "compliance first-mover" dividends. They include iFlytek (Spark Agent end-to-end platform), Alibaba (Bailian Agent), Baidu (Wenxin Agent), Tencent (Hunyuan Agent), Zhipu (Qingyan Agent), and DeepSeek (Agent team). They will not only enjoy regulatory first-mover advantage, but will also win agent transformation contracts from government, SOEs and large enterprise clients.

Tier 2: Vertical Agent Application Vendors

With unified rules in place, demand for agent transformation will explode across manufacturing, enterprise office, finance, government, healthcare and more. Yonyou, Kingsoft Office, Weaver Network and other B2B SaaS leaders benefit most directly; industrial players like Supcon Technology, Digital China, and edge-side players like ThunderSoft will gain scaled orders.

Tier 3: Upstream AI Infrastructure

Concurrent multi-agent collaboration will significantly increase consumption of compute, storage and network resources. AI servers (Inspur, Sugon), domestic AI chips, and AI middleware and vector databases (Transwarp, Beyondsoft Information) will enter a new expansion cycle.

IV. Deeper Logic: The Global Significance of China's AI Governance Paradigm

Viewed globally, the 40 + 7 standards together represent a uniquely Chinese AI governance path, distinct from both Europe and the United States.

The EU follows risk-tiered regulation (the AI Act), centered on "ex-ante compliance for high-risk AI systems"; the US relies on industry self-regulation plus executive orders, anchored in "top vendor restraint plus critical tech controls"; China chose "industry-guided-by-standards" — supplying national standards so that regulation becomes an accelerator of innovation, not a roadblock.

The key advantage of this path is that standards are not prohibitions but "enabling rules" — they reduce R&D and coordination costs industry-wide, letting smaller firms compete on a unified foundation. This is also why open-source ecosystems thrive in China — from DeepSeek, Qwen and GLM to Zhipu and Moonshot, the rise of China's open-source camp is structurally aligned with the philosophy of "governing AI through standards, not licenses."

"AI governance is not about restricting AI, but about letting AI run faster within trustworthy rules. The simultaneous release of 40+ AI standards and 7 agent interconnection standards marks the evolution of our framework from 'point-by-point regulation' to 'full-stack systems.'"

— Fan Kefeng, Deputy Director, China Electronics Standardization Institute

V. Conclusion: From "Rule Follower" to "Rule Supplier"

If the most striking achievement of China's AI industry in 2024-2025 was DeepSeek's cost revolution and open-source models' global breakthrough, then the most pivotal turning point of 2026 may be "China transitioning from rule-follower to rule-supplier in AI."

The 40 standards plus the 7 agent interconnection standards are not isolated policy events, but a watershed as China's AI industry moves from "unbridled growth" to "regulated deep cultivation." While the world still debates "how to regulate AI," China has delivered a complete answer: rule-supply as the hub, regulation as the accelerator.

Over the next 12-18 months, three things will unfold: first, the identity code standard becomes mandatory, forcing all vendors to retrofit for compliance; second, local compliance subsidies roll out, accelerating large-scale agent deployment; third, Chinese national standards begin conversion into ISO/IEC international standards, claiming global discourse. This quiet rule revolution will profoundly reshape the global AI landscape for the next decade.
