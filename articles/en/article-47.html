1. President Xi's WAIC 2026 Keynote: Five Core Messages

At 9:00 AM on July 18, President Xi Jinping took the stage at the WAIC 2026 main forum. This was Xi's first in-person keynote at WAIC since its inaugural 2018 edition—a strong signal in itself that WAIC has been elevated from an industry conference to a national strategic platform.

The approximately 3,000-character speech covered five core messages:

First, AI is a 'strategic technology leading this round of technological revolution and industrial transformation.' Xi positioned AI as a 'strategic technology,' an upgrade from previous descriptions of AI as an 'enabling technology.' This signals that China will allocate more resources at the national strategic level for AI development, including enhanced fiscal and tax support, talent cultivation, and infrastructure investment.

Second, 'people-centered, safety-grounded, and cooperation-oriented.' These twelve characters form the three principles of China's AI governance. 'People-centered' emphasizes the inclusivity of AI development—AI must not widen the digital divide or exacerbate social inequality. 'Safety-grounded' responds to global concerns about AI safety risks—from algorithmic bias to deepfakes, data privacy to military applications. 'Cooperation-oriented' directly points to the motivation behind WAICO—China seeks not 'unipolar dominance' in AI but 'multilateral co-governance.'

Third, opposition to 'any form of decoupling and technology blockade.' This was Xi's most direct response to the trend of 'de-Sinicization' in the AI field on an international stage. The speech made clear that AI technology development requires global collaboration, and any attempt to exclude China from the AI innovation chain is 'neither feasible nor wise.' This statement was cited repeatedly by delegates from multiple countries and became one of the most discussed topics after the opening ceremony.

Fourth, the announcement of WAICO. This was the core deliverable of the speech. Xi emphasized that WAICO will be an 'open, inclusive, equal, and mutual-learning' intergovernmental organization, welcoming all nations to join. The 29 founding members span Asia, Europe, Africa, South America, and Oceania, reflecting broad geographic representation.

Fifth, China's willingness to share AI development dividends with all nations. Xi pledged that China will provide AI technology assistance, talent training, and infrastructure support to developing countries through the WAICO platform, helping to narrow the global AI development gap—a commitment consistent with the 'people-centered' principle.

• Three principles: People-centered, safety-grounded, cooperation-oriented

2. WAICO: Architecture and Significance of the World's First AI Intergovernmental Organization

WAICO's establishment was not overnight. According to diplomatic officials involved in the preparations, WAICO's groundwork began as early as the second half of 2025, going through multiple rounds of intergovernmental consultations and treaty text negotiations. The 'Shanghai Agreement' signed by 29 founding members on July 18 is a legal document of approximately 30 pages, detailing WAICO's purpose, organizational structure, decision-making mechanisms, and operational rules.

Organizational Structure

WAICO adopts a four-tier structure: 'Assembly—Council—Secretariat—Specialized Committees.' The Assembly is the supreme authority, convening every two years with representatives from all member states. The Council consists of 15 member states (3-year terms), responsible for daily decision-making. The Secretariat is headquartered in Shanghai as the permanent executive body. Specialized Committees are established by technical domain (e.g., large model safety, computing standards, data governance, talent development) and composed of experts from member states.

Core Functions

WAICO's core functions can be summarized in four areas: Rule Coordination—promoting alignment and compatibility of global AI governance rules, reducing fragmentation; Standard Mutual Recognition—facilitating cross-border mutual recognition of AI technical standards (safety evaluation, data annotation, model evaluation); Safety Co-building—establishing a global AI safety incident information-sharing mechanism and emergency response framework; Capacity Building—helping developing countries build AI capabilities through technology transfer, talent training, and infrastructure assistance.

Strategic Significance

WAICO's establishment carries multiple layers of strategic significance. Globally, it is the most important multilateral AI governance mechanism to date—while there have been the UN AI Advisory Body, the G7 Hiroshima AI Process, and the OECD AI Principles, none were dedicated intergovernmental organizations. WAICO fills this gap.

For China, WAICO represents an identity leap from 'AI technology power' to 'AI rule maker.' Over the past decade, China's AI technical capabilities have risen rapidly—from paper counts and patent filings to model performance, multiple indicators now rank among the global top tier. However, in international rule-setting, China's voice has not matched its capabilities. WAICO's establishment is a critical step in translating AI technical strength into rule-making influence.

WAICO Key Facts:

• Name: World AI Cooperation Organization (WAICO)

• Headquarters: Shanghai, China

• Founding Members: 29 (spanning five continents)

• Legal Document: Shanghai Agreement (~30 pages)

• Structure: Assembly→Council (15 members)→Secretariat→Specialized Committees

• Core Functions: Rule Coordination, Standard Mutual Recognition, Safety Co-building, Capacity Building

Action 7: AI Industry Empowerment. Drive large-scale AI model deployment across manufacturing, agriculture, healthcare, and education, cultivating 1,000 AI demonstration application scenarios by 2028.

Action 8: Innovation Ecosystem Building. Establish 10 national-level AI innovation platforms, encouraging independent innovation in AI basic research, core algorithms, and critical hardware.

4. From 'Tech Catching Up' to 'Rule Setting': China's Strategic AI Transformation

The highest value of WAIC 2026 lies not in the dazzling AI products on display but in a deeper strategic signal—China's AI is completing its identity transformation from 'technology follower' to 'rule maker.'

Over the past decade, China's AI story has been one of 'catching up.' From the 2017 'New Generation AI Development Plan' to the global attention on DeepSeek V4 in 2024, China's AI has steadily narrowed the gap with the United States in technical capabilities. But technical catch-up has a ceiling—when technical capabilities approach the frontier, competition is no longer determined by technology alone, but by rules, standards, and ecosystems.

WAICO's establishment and the 'AI Cooperative Development Action Plan' mark China's AI strategy entering a 'rule-setting' phase. The 29 founding members signing the Shanghai Agreement means China has gained 'agenda-setting power' in AI governance—whoever sets the topics of discussion holds the starting point of discourse. The NDRC's 8 action plans demonstrate China's 'institutional innovation capability' in AI infrastructure, open-source ecosystems, and data supply—China's institutional advantage is extending from 'concentrating resources to accomplish major tasks' to 'concentrating resources to set rules.'

Of course, the journey from 'rule maker' to 'rule leader' remains long. The 29 founding members of WAICO do not include major AI powers like the United States, Japan, or the United Kingdom. China will need to attract more countries to join the WAICO framework through tangible results and an open, inclusive posture. Meanwhile, the international contest over AI governance rules will not end with WAICO's establishment—the EU's AI Act, the US AI Executive Order, and the UK AI Safety Summit are all advancing in parallel. China must find its position within multiple governance frameworks.

But regardless, July 18, 2026, is a date worth remembering. On this day, China's AI is no longer just a 'follower'—it has begun to set the rules.
