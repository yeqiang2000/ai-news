1. An Unprecedented Concept

On June 6, 2026, Reuters, CCTV News, and R&D World simultaneously confirmed that senior U.S. government officials had held preliminary discussions with major American AI companies about the possibility of government equity stakes in AI firms. The core of discussions centered on companies "voluntarily transferring shares to the government," with investment returns subsequently used for public purposes.

This is not an offhand remark. According to sources, OpenAI CEO Sam Altman first proposed this concept to Trump in early 2025, and has revisited discussions with senior government officials in recent weeks, framing it as a means to "more broadly distribute AI-related economic benefits to the public."

Trump himself stated publicly on June 5 that his team would "look into" the concept. That same day, R&D World framed the trend more bluntly: Trump's AI push is transforming the government into "reviewer, military contractor, and potential shareholder."

If this materializes, it would be the first time in U.S. history the federal government systematically holds equity in private tech companies. It would fundamentally reshape the power relationship between Washington and Silicon Valley.

2. Why AI? Why Now?

Government equity stakes in private enterprises are not without precedent, but never in the technology sector — and never under the banner of "universal sharing of AI benefits."

The driving logic behind this concept has three layers:

First, AI is strategic infrastructure. In 2026, AI is no longer a single industry's technology upgrade — it has become a pervasive, cross-sector infrastructure touching national defense, finance, healthcare, education, and energy. When AI companies' decisions directly affect national security and economic stability, the government cannot remain a bystander.

Second, unprecedented capital concentration. In H1 2026, Anthropic reached a $965 billion valuation, OpenAI reportedly surpassed $1.2 trillion, and Alphabet launched an $85 billion dedicated AI financing round. Global AI capital is concentrating into a handful of companies at an unprecedented speed. This level of concentration exceeds what traditional antitrust frameworks can address.

Third, the fairness of benefit distribution. Value created by AI is accelerating toward capital, while labor displacement effects grow increasingly significant. Government equity is a direct value redistribution mechanism — if government-shareholding corresponds to a portion of AI company profits, those funds flow into public coffers rather than remaining entirely with private shareholders.

3. International Precedents: Sovereign Wealth Funds and National Champion Models

Globally, government ownership of enterprises is not new, but the models vary considerably:

Norway's Government Pension Fund Global (GPFG) — the world's largest sovereign wealth fund, holding approximately 1.5% of global listed company shares, including many tech companies. But GPFG's core principles are passive investment, non-interference in management, and extreme transparency, with all returns flowing to Norwegian citizens' pensions.

Singapore's Temasek — holds stakes in Singapore's core enterprises including telecom, aviation, and finance, widely seen as a model of the "national champion" approach. Temasek's governance maintains relative independence, but government influence is substantively present through board seats.

China's State Enterprise Model — the state directly controls or holds stakes in key sectors, including the three major carriers, four major banks, and principal tech platforms. The government deeply involves itself in operations through party organizations, personnel appointments, and strategic guidance.

U.S. government equity in AI differs from all three models. This is not a passive market-purchased position formed after the fact — it is proactively acquiring equity in exchange for policy favors during a company's early stages. It is not establishing an independently operated sovereign fund — it could mean the executive branch directly holds shares. Transparency and governance independence will be the most significant institutional challenges.

4. Conflicts of Interest: When the Referee Also Plays

When the government simultaneously serves as AI regulator and invested party, it creates the most direct conflict of interest. Several scenarios illustrate the severity:

Antitrust enforcement dilemma. If the DOJ is investigating an AI company's market monopoly, and the Treasury holds 5% of that company's shares, how are the rigor and impartiality of antitrust enforcement guaranteed?

Security review paradox. The White House currently requires AI companies to voluntarily submit their most powerful models to the government for cybersecurity testing 30 days before release. If the government is a shareholder, does this "voluntary" submission retain any substantive meaning? Will companies reduce risk-disclosure transparency out of concern for stock price impacts?

Competitive distortion. If the government holds shares in OpenAI but not Anthropic, does OpenAI gain an unfair advantage in obtaining government contracts? Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei implicitly addressed this in an early June interview, stating any policy measure changing the competitive landscape requires "extreme transparency and reciprocity."

Information asymmetry. As a shareholder, the government will gain access to AI companies' internal financial and strategic information. Could this information improperly flow to regulators, contract evaluation committees, or Congressional members? Where are the firewalls?

This is not theoretical. In 2025, the FDIC faced similar role conflicts during the Silicon Valley Bank collapse — simultaneously serving as deposit insurer,接管人 (receiver), and creditor. AI conflicts of interest will only be more complex.

5. The Illusion of "Voluntary Transfer"

The current discussions frame the concept as "voluntarily transferring shares to the government." But how much substance does "voluntary" carry in a context of power asymmetry?

AI companies depend on three government resources: federal procurement contracts (the Defense Department is one of the largest AI customers), export control exemptions (chip export licenses can determine a company's survival), and regulatory tolerance (federal preemption is freezing state laws). In the face of these chips, is the "voluntary" transfer negotiation table truly level?

Moreover, the 269-page Great American AI Act is under Congressional discussion, containing federal preemption clauses, mandatory audit requirements, and twice-yearly compliance reviews. An unnamed AI company government relations lead told media: "You don't want to be the only company not transferring equity to the government."

This structural coercion makes "voluntary" more of a political rhetorical device than an institutional safeguard.

6. Paths Ahead: Three Scenarios

Scenario 1: Sovereign AI Fund Model. The government establishes an independently operated sovereign AI fund, passively holding AI company shares in the manner of Norway's GPFG, with fund returns entering a dedicated public account. This offers the highest transparency and governance independence, with minimal market distortion. But it requires Congressional authorization, and passage is difficult in the current politically polarized environment.

Scenario 2: Policy Quid Pro Quo Model. The government makes equity transfer a condition when granting federal AI contracts, export licenses, or regulatory exemptions. Like: "You want this defense contract? Give the government 2% equity." This model is flexible but opaque, prone to rent-seeking, and lacks Congressional oversight.

Scenario 3: Universal AI Dividend Model. Following the Alaska Permanent Fund model (distributing oil revenue dividends to all state residents annually), the government distributes AI equity returns directly to all citizens as an "AI dividend." Altman has long championed universal basic income (UBI); the AI dividend is its policy embodiment. But the fiscal sustainability of this model heavily depends on AI companies' profitability — and currently, the vast majority of AI companies are still unprofitable.

Whichever path, all must answer one fundamental question: Who supervises the supervisors?

Conclusion: Who Protects Those Who Are Not Shareholders?

The U.S. government considering equity stakes in AI companies is less an economic decision than a political proposition about the boundaries of power. When the state shifts from rule-maker to game participant, when the regulator's and regulated's interests intertwine, do traditional checks and balances still function?

AI's rise is forcing every nation to redefine the relationship between government and markets. America has chosen an unprecedented path — not simply strengthening regulation, not deregulating the market, but directly sitting down at the table. Whether this plays out well depends on whether institutional design can keep pace with ambition.

History tells us that once power and capital combine, they rarely recede voluntarily. If America goes down this road, the world will be watching: when the state becomes an AI shareholder, who protects those who are not shareholders?
