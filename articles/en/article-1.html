NVIDIA GTC 2026: Three AI Chips Launched, Agent Era Fully Arrives

From data centers to consumer PCs, from cloud to edge — NVIDIA's AI empire is accelerating its expansion

📅 Published June 3, 2026 📖 8 min read 🏷️ Chips & Computing
Key Takeaway: CEO Jensen Huang dropped three "bombshells" at COMPUTEX Taipei: Vera Rubin officially entered mass production, RTX Spark entered the PC market, and Vera CPU took direct aim at Intel. From data centers to consumer terminals, from cloud to edge, NVIDIA's AI empire is accelerating its expansion at full speed.

🚀 1. Vera Rubin Officially Enters Mass Production: End of Delay Rumors

Over the past few months, the market was filled with doubts about whether NVIDIA's flagship AI platform Vera Rubin could be delivered on schedule. Jensen Huang's public confirmation at this conference completely dispelled these concerns.

💡 Key Points

The Vera Rubin NVL72 GPU system has officially entered mass production, with first customers including OpenAI, Anthropic, and other leading AI companies.

The Vera Rubin platform consists of five dedicated racks operating as an integrated AI supercomputer, incorporating the Vera Rubin NVL72 GPU system, Vera CPU, BlueField-4 STX storage, and Spectrum-6 networking. Compared to the previous generation Blackwell, this platform delivers 10x agent throughput at scale.

📋 Vera Rubin Platform Core Specifications

  • GPU: Vera Rubin NVL72
  • CPU: Vera CPU (designed for agent AI)
  • Storage: BlueField-4 STX
  • Networking: Spectrum-6
  • Throughput improvement: 10x (vs Blackwell)

💻 2. RTX Spark: NVIDIA Enters the PC Chip Battlefield

If Vera Rubin was expected, then RTX Spark's announcement shocked the entire tech world — NVIDIA officially entered the consumer PC chip market, directly challenging the x86 monopoly that Intel and AMD have dominated for decades.

"The scale of this PC industry revolution is comparable to the evolution from feature phones to smartphones. All next-generation PCs can natively run agent AI."
— Jensen Huang, COMPUTEX 2026

RTX Spark uses ARM architecture, directly connecting a Blackwell RTX GPU with a 20-core Grace CPU via NVIDIA NVLink C2C, manufactured using TSMC's 3nm process. This "super chip" has the following capabilities:

  • 1 petaflop of AI computing power
  • Up to 128GB unified memory
  • Can render 3D scenes exceeding 90GB
  • Can edit 12K 4:2:2 format video
  • Can run 120-billion-parameter large models locally
  • Can run AAA games at 1440p resolution at over 100fps

PC Chip Market Landscape Shifts

OEM manufacturers including Microsoft, Dell, HP, ASUS, Lenovo, and MSI have confirmed they will launch new products featuring RTX Spark this fall. Analysts point out that this is not only NVIDIA's victory but another landmark event in the continued rise of ARM architecture.

🧠 3. Vera CPU: Direct Challenge to Intel and AMD

The most surprising announcement for analysts at this conference was the strategic elevation of NVIDIA's CPU business. Jensen Huang explicitly stated: "In the agent AI era, the CPU has become the bottleneck constraining GPU performance."

The Vera CPU is specifically built for agent AI workloads, delivering 3x performance improvement over x86 competitors in SQL operations, 6x improvement in data processing, and approximately 1.8x overall performance improvement in common agent tools such as Python, code analysis, and compilation.

💡 Key Change

Historically, NVIDIA sold approximately two GPUs for every CPU. But with the explosion in agent AI demand, the CPU-to-GPU ratio has approached 1:1. This means NVIDIA's system value will receive a substantial boost.

🚗 4. Alpamayo 2: The "Virtual Driving School" for Autonomous Driving

NVIDIA's布局 in autonomous driving also caught attention. Alpamayo 2 is an open 32-billion-parameter vision-language-action reasoning model (Reasoning VLA) that supports L4 autonomous driving development.

Core Breakthrough: Explainability

Traditional end-to-end autonomous driving models are like a "silent veteran driver," while Alpamayo 2 is like a "chatty driver" — the vehicle can use natural language to explain each decision in real time:

  • "Micro-adjusting left because a stationary vehicle ahead is blocking the lane"
  • "Yielding to a vehicle cutting in from the left"
  • "Stopping to yield before crossing traffic"

This "chain of thought" externalization capability is clearly more conducive to gaining public trust.

AI Gym + OmniDreams: Simulation Training Loop

NVIDIA simultaneously launched AlpaGym (open-source reinforcement learning framework) and OmniDreams (generative world model), together forming the "virtual driving school" for autonomous driving:

  • AlpaGym: Allows AI drivers to experience millions of extreme road conditions in "parallel universes," reducing the cost of mistakes to zero
  • OmniDreams: Generates realistic closed-loop assisted driving scenarios, supporting large-scale simulation of rare long-tail driving scenarios

This "simulation-training-deployment" closed loop means: car companies no longer need to deploy thousands of test vehicles to accumulate data, but can complete over 90% of edge case verification in digital twin environments.

📊 Deep Analysis: NVIDIA's "Full-Stack AI" Ambitions

Based on the products announced at this COMPUTEX, NVIDIA's strategic intent is very clear:

  1. Hardware level: Expanding from GPUs to CPUs, forming a complete AI computing platform
  2. Scenario level: Extending from data centers to PCs, smart cars, robots, and other terminals
  3. Ecosystem level: Building a developer ecosystem through open-source tools (AlpaGym, H2Plus humanoid robot solutions)
