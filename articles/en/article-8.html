Trillion-Dollar AI IPO Wave: Historic Listing Surge or Bubble Signal?

SpaceX targeting June 12 listing at $750B, Anthropic reportedly filing S-1, OpenAI year-end target — combined ~$200B in financing goals. When "Big Short" protagonist Michael Burry sounds the alarm, when indexes shorten inclusion periods, and when retirement accounts are forced to take these shares, are we witnessing the peak of the AI era — or just the peak of AI hype?

Combined financing targets reach approximately $200 billion — nearly double the previous annual IPO financing record of $119.3 billion set in 2021. But as Michael Burry warns "nothing in SpaceX's S-1 can justify a $1 trillion valuation," and as indices fast-track inclusion, the question is no longer whether this is historic — but whether it is a bubble.

1. $200 Billion Capital Flood

In June 2026, the U.S. capital market is witnessing an unprecedented IPO wave. SpaceX updated its prospectus on June 10, planning to issue 556 million shares at $135 each, raising $75 billion at a $1.77 trillion valuation — easily shattering Saudi Aramco's 2019 global IPO record of $29.4 billion.

Simultaneously, Anthropic secretly filed a draft S-1 registration statement with the SEC on June 1, targeting a formal listing in September. OpenAI also plans to go public before year-end. Market estimates suggest each company could raise approximately $60 billion.

The combined financing target of approximately $200 billion nearly doubles the $119.3 billion annual IPO financing record set in 2021.

To accelerate inclusion of these giant IPOs into major indices and attract passive investment capital, Nasdaq has shortened the post-IPO inclusion waiting period to 15 trading days, FTSE Russell to 5 days, with S&P Dow Jones considering similar adjustments. This means hundreds of millions of retirement accounts will be forced to hold these stocks within extremely short timeframes.

2. Astronomical Valuations and the Gravity of Reality

Valuation is the most controversial core of this IPO wave. SpaceX generated approximately $19 billion in revenue in 2025 while posting a loss of approximately $5 billion. At a $1.77 trillion valuation, the price-to-sales ratio exceeds 90x. Renowned investor Michael Burry — whose subprime mortgage short was dramatized in "The Big Short" — publicly stated that after reviewing SpaceX's S-1, "nothing in it can support a $1 trillion valuation, let alone $2 trillion."

Investment research firm New Constructs CEO David Trainer was more blunt, calling the current IPO wave "potentially one of the biggest scams in history." He noted that unlike the dot-com bubble era, today's investors may have little choice — rapid index inclusion means millions of retirement accounts will be forced to hold these stocks regardless of valuation reasonableness.

Anthropic's valuation is equally staggering. At $61.5 billion in March 2025, it surged to $965 billion following its Series H financing in May 2026 — nearly a 16x increase in just over a year. At an annualized revenue exceeding $44 billion, the price-to-sales ratio is approximately 22x. While significantly lower than SpaceX's 90x, it still far exceeds traditional tech company valuation levels.

3. Mirror of History: IPO Waves and Market Peaks

Historical data provides a sobering reference for the current frenzy. Research by University of Florida professor Jay R. Ritter found that between 1980 and 2024, IPO stocks underperformed the broader market by an average of 20 percentage points over three years. Companies with price-to-sales ratios exceeding 40x underperformed by a staggering 58 percentage points. SpaceX's 90x price-to-sales ratio suggests potentially massive downward pressure ahead.

Truist strategist Sam Grelck's data shows large IPOs typically perform well initially but turn negative at the 6-month and 12-month horizons. Among 30 large IPO samples, 19 experienced at least 50% maximum drawdowns within their first year of listing.

More notable is the correlation between IPO waves and market peaks. In 1999, U.S. IPO activity surged to 476 deals raising $64.7 billion — nearly double the previous year — followed by the dot-com bubble burst. In 2008, average IPO size reached $1.084 billion, approximately five times that of prior years — coinciding with the global financial crisis. In 2021, IPO financing set a record of $119.3 billion — followed by a bear market in 2022.

TS Lombard economist Dario Perkins posed a pointed question: If AI truly is transformative, why are these companies so eager to share ownership now? The answer may be — insiders believe valuations are near their peak, and they want to exit while conditions remain favorable.

4. Liquidity Squeeze and Market Impact

The scale of these giant IPOs is triggering liquidity concerns. Fund manager cash levels have dropped to 3.9%, near historical lows, while equity allocation is at a four-year high. This means many investors are already fully invested, making large capital rotations potentially unavoidable.

Investors may need to sell existing holdings to participate in these IPOs, potentially triggering selling pressure in other market sectors — particularly large-cap tech stocks. Over the past month, the Roundhill Magnificent Seven ETF rose only 3%, underperforming the S&P 500's 4.48% and Nasdaq Composite's 5.94%, while aerospace, telecom, and even Bitcoin showed signs of capital outflows.

MIT Digital Economy Initiative's Paul Kedrosky warned many current holdings may face "mechanical selling pressure." Carson Group portfolio manager Blake Anderson suggested these shifts may already reflect investors reallocating capital in anticipation of the SpaceX IPO.

5. AI's Uniqueness: Is This Time Really Different?

Not all analysts are bearish. Renaissance Capital senior strategist Matt Kennedy believes Wall Street has ample capital ready to deploy into AI. "If Anthropic could raise $65 billion in the private market, it should be able to do the same in the public market without disrupting broader markets."

The bull case rests on a key distinction: AI genuinely differs from many dot-com bubble-era companies. Anthropic generated $4.8 billion in Q1 2026 revenue, with Q2 expected to surge to $10.9 billion, and is on track to achieve its first quarterly profit. This is fundamentally different from 1999 internet companies with no revenue.

But even so, the speed of valuation expansion still exceeds what fundamentals can explain. Anthropic went from $380 billion to $965 billion in just three months. OpenAI reached an $852 billion valuation in under ten years. This pace itself is a signal — not of technological progress, but of capital impatience.

💡 Key Risks for Ordinary Investors

1. Index passive holding risk: If you hold S&P 500 or Nasdaq 100 index funds, these giant IPOs will become major holdings within extremely short timeframes. You can reduce exposure before inclusion, but that contradicts the premise of passive investing.

2. Valuation anchor absent: These AI companies lack comparable listed peers. When P/S ratios range from 22x to 90x, "reasonable valuation" itself becomes a question without consensus.

3. Policy black swan: Anthropic remains on the Pentagon's "supply chain risk" list. If it cannot be removed before listing, institutional investor buying appetite will be significantly impacted. U.S. government equity stake discussions further compound regulatory uncertainty.

6. Conclusion: Technology Revolution and Capital Carnival at the Same Crossroads

The 2026 AI IPO wave is where technological revolution meets capital carnival. Every technological revolution in history has been accompanied by capital bubbles — railroads, the internet, clean energy — but bubbles themselves do not mean the technology direction was wrong. The question is: when the bubble bursts, who pays the price? This time, rapid index inclusion mechanisms have forced millions of ordinary investors' retirement accounts to the front line.

Caution is not a rejection of AI — it is a respect for history.
