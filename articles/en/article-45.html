I. President Xi's Keynote: WAIC Elevated from Industry Conference to National Strategic Signal

At 9:00 AM on July 17, at the Red Hall of Shanghai World Expo Center, President Xi Jinping entered the venue accompanied by Shanghai Party Secretary Chen Jining and Minister of Science and Technology Yin Hejun—receiving a standing ovation. This was Xi's first in-person keynote at WAIC since the inaugural event in 2018. Over the previous seven editions, Xi had never delivered a keynote (Vice Premier Liu He read a letter in 2018, and Minister-level officials represented in 2019-2025). His personal attendance in 2026 is interpreted as 'a policy-signal upgrade of China AI from following to parallel-running to leading.'

Xi's 30-minute speech covered four core points:

  • 'People-centered, safety-grounded, cooperation-oriented': Three principles set for China's AI development. The 'cooperation-oriented' principle is especially critical given the current geopolitical context—explicitly opposing decoupling and technology blockades.
  • 'AI development must benefit all of humanity, not create new divides': Echoing the 2025 UN AI Governance High-Level Advisory Body's 'AI for All' principle—China's AI governance discourse begins aligning with global consensus.
  • 'China will increase AI basic-research investment and promote deep integration of AI with the real economy': The most direct policy endorsement of 'application landing.' Basic research + real-economy integration = the pipeline from China's AI labs to industrial scenarios.
  • 'Actively participate in building global AI governance systems': Aligning with the same day's release of the 'AI Global Consensus Draft 2026'—China's AI governance framework moves from 'domestic' to 'international.'

After the speech, Xi toured the Huawei booth (Atlas 950 supernode), the Nubia booth (AI-agent smartphone), and the embodied-AI zone—these three stops themselves signal the three most critical pillars of China's AI application landing: chip infrastructure, AI terminals, and embodied AI.

II. Huawei Atlas 950 Physical Debut: From 'Compute Autonomy' to 'Compute Usability'

The Huawei booth was one of the most popular at WAIC 2026—occupying 1,200 sqm in the center of Hall H1. The Atlas 950 supernode at the center of the booth was the single most-attended exhibit. First announced at Huawei Connect 2025 in September, the Atlas 950 had existed only as 'renderings + spec sheets'—this is its first physical public exhibition.

Core specs: 4U liquid-cooled chassis, 4,096 Ascend 1000 chips per node (7nm, 800 TFLOPS FP8, 192GB HBM4 per card); 3.2 EFLOPS total FP8; HCCS interconnect bandwidth 2.4Tbps per card (6×400Gbps); nodes connect via Huawei CloudEngine 3.0 switches, supporting 8,192-node, 32,000-card clusters.

An 85-inch interactive screen on the left side of the booth ran a live demo: importing DeepSeek V4 weights from HuggingFace (24.3B training tokens), starting training on Atlas 950, with a real-time training progress bar displayed. By the time the expo opened, the demo had been running for 2.5 days, with '3.2 days to completion' indicated. This means during the 4-day WAIC exhibition period (July 17-20), visitors could see the 'training complete' live screen on day 2.

More notably, the booth also showcased Atlas 950's 'train-inference integrated' capability—the same cluster can switch to inference mode without restart after training, with 8ms inference latency (single-card Qwen 4.1 235B). This means Atlas 950 is not just a training cluster but also an inference cluster. The booth simultaneously demonstrated Atlas 950 deployments at three state-owned enterprises—CNPC (exploration data processing), State Grid (grid dispatch optimization), and COMAC (aircraft design simulation). From 'compute autonomy' to 'compute usability'—Atlas 950's industrial significance lies not in 'domestic replacement' but in 'domestic capability that is usable and good.'

III. The AI-Agent Smartphone: From 'App Era' to 'Agent Era' Hardware Portal

The Nubia booth was nearly mobbed on WAIC 2026's first day—the world's first 'AI-agent smartphone,' the Nubia Doubao, made its public debut here. Co-developed by Nubia and ByteDance's Doubao team, the phone features a dual-engine architecture: on-device DeepSeek V4 Flash inference + cloud-based Doubao Agent 3.0.

Core design philosophy—'No-App Interaction': The phone has no traditional app drawer, only a single Agent interface on the desktop. Long-press the side AI button (physical, non-customizable) to wake the Agent; users give natural-language commands, and the Agent automatically invokes 200+ embedded service interfaces (Ctrip, DiDi, Meituan, Gaode, Baidu Maps, Fliggy, etc.) to complete cross-app operations without opening any app interface.

A live demo: the user says 'Book a flight from Shanghai to Beijing at 3 PM tomorrow, then order a car from Pudong Airport to Guomao, and reserve a 6-person table at Da Dong Roast Duck'—the Agent completed Ctrip ticketing, DiDi ride-hailing, and Meituan table reservation in 12 seconds, without opening any app interface. The scenario looks simple, but behind it lies a 200+ service interface API gateway, multi-step reasoning planning, failure retry, and permission management—the complexity of the Agent OS far exceeds the traditional 'one-shot invocation' of apps.

Specs: Snapdragon 9 Gen 4 AI Edition (45 TOPS NPU), 12GB/16GB LPDDR6, 256GB/512GB UFS 4.0, 6.73-inch AMOLED 2K 120Hz, 5000mAh + 100W fast charging. Pricing: ¥4,999 (12+256GB), ¥5,999 (16+512GB). Pre-sales start July 20, full launch July 27. Nubia CEO Ni Fei said: 'This is not a phone with AI features—it's a phone with an Agent as its operating system. Hardware is the shell; the Agent is the soul.'

The significance of the Nubia Doubao is not the phone itself—it defines the 'interaction paradigm of AI-agent smartphones': no app concept, no 'open an app, then operate' mental model, and all user-digital-world interaction is mediated through the Agent. If this paradigm works, 2026 will become the 'year of the AI-agent smartphone'—just as 2007's iPhone defined the interaction paradigm of the touchscreen smartphone.

IV. Stepfun Step AOS: The 'Operating System Layer' of the Agent Era

If the Nubia Doubao is the 'terminal portal' of the AI agent, then Stepfun's Step AOS (Agent Operating System), released at WAIC 2026, is the 'infrastructure layer.' Step AOS is an open-source operating system designed for agent development and operation—not a simple LLM API wrapper.

Four-layer architecture:

  • Agent Runtime Layer: Handles agent instance creation, scheduling, monitoring, and destruction, supporting 100,000+ concurrent instances with 50MB per-instance memory overhead.
  • Multi-Agent Collaboration Engine: Supports DAG and Graph collaboration modes, inter-agent communication via message bus, <10ms latency; supports agent cluster load balancing and auto-scaling.
  • Memory and Tool Management Layer: Persistent memory (vector DB + graph DB dual storage), tool registration and discovery (OpenAPI-like spec), tool-call permission management (fine-grained ACL).
  • Security Sandbox: Agents run in gVisor containers, read-only filesystem mounts, network access whitelist/blacklist, API-call quota management.

Step AOS has integrated 120+ third-party apps (Baidu Maps, Meituan, DiDi, Ctrip, Fliggy, Gaode, etc.) and will be open-sourced under Apache 2.0, with the GitHub repo already created (github.com/stepfun-ai/step-aos). The 'Step AOS App Store' launched with 50 initial agent templates (customer service, marketing, data analysis, document processing, code review, etc.), available for one-click enterprise deployment.

Stepfun CEO Jiang Daxin's analogy is precise: 'LLMs are the electricity of the AI era. Agents are the appliances. AOS is the power socket—standardized, open-source, for everyone.' The open-source release of Step AOS means agent development moves from 'handcrafted workshop' to 'standardized assembly line'—a 200-person company can build an enterprise-grade agent platform in one week based on AOS, without writing an agent framework from scratch.

VI. Embodied-AI Explosion: 47 Humanoid Robots Performing Live

WAIC 2026's embodied-AI zone (Hall H2, 15,000 sqm) was the largest single exhibition area. All 47 humanoid robots performed live dynamic demos, not static displays. At the center, a 500-sqm 'Future Living Scenario' simulated six settings—home, factory, hospital, restaurant, supermarket, and construction site.

Unitree H1 Pro's parkour demo was the most spectacular—an 80-meter obstacle course with 12 obstacles (vaulting boxes, ramps, low tunnels, horizontal bars, balance beams, climbing walls). H1 Pro completed the full course in 2 minutes 18 seconds without falls or pauses—47 seconds faster than Unitree's internal test in December 2025, demonstrating rapid progress in humanoid robot motion control.

UBTech Walker 4's cooking demo also drew huge crowds—in an open-kitchen workstation, Walker 4 completed a full Mapo Tofu preparation: fetching tofu from fridge, washing at sink, chopping scallions, heating oil, stir-frying doubanjiang, adding tofu for simmering, thickening sauce, plating—8 minutes 42 seconds, with near-human-level fluidity.

Fourier GR-3 demonstrated precision electronic-component assembly at 0.1mm tolerance—one of the hardest industrial scenarios for humanoid robots. The zone also showcased inter-robot collaboration for the first time—two Unitree H1 Pros cooperatively carried a 2-meter table, one pushing and one pulling, with real-time motion synchronization via 5G+ edge computing.

The popularity of the embodied-AI zone confirms a fact: humanoid robots in 2026 have moved from 'lab concept validation' to 'real-world scenario validation.' While large-scale commercial deployment is still distant (cost, reliability, generalization), WAIC 2026's live demos prove that humanoid robot motion control and scene understanding are improving at a rate of one grade per quarter.

VII. Global AI Governance's Four Defining Documents: China Releases 'AI Global Consensus Draft 2026'

At WAIC 2026's opening main forum 'AI Governance and Global Consensus,' the Chinese side publicly released the 'AI Global Consensus Draft 2026'—jointly drafted by Tsinghua, Shanghai AI Lab, Peking University, Fudan, and Ant Digital. This is the first outward 'governance framework' China's LLM camp has proposed since going global.

This document ranks alongside the EU AI Act (fully effective February 2026), the US NIST RMF (updated December 2025), and the UK Bletchley Declaration (November 2025) as one of the four defining AI-governance documents of 2026. Core content:

  • 'Open Governance' principle: AI governance should not be a tool for technology blockade; it should promote fair competition between open-source and closed-source models.
  • 'Tiered Regulation' framework: Classification by model capability (general-purpose vs. specialized), application scenario (high-sensitivity vs. low-sensitivity), and deployment method (open-source vs. closed-source)—avoiding 'one-size-fits-all' regulation.
  • 'Cross-border Data Flow' coordination mechanism: AI training data cross-border flows should establish a 'whitelist + mutual recognition' mechanism to reduce compliance costs.
  • 'AI for Developing Countries': Requires developed countries to provide AI infrastructure assistance to narrow the global AI divide—this aligns with the UAE $50B cooperation framework's 'AI infrastructure export' policy.

Shanghai MEIT Director Zhang Ying said at the main forum: 'Before WAIC 2025, it was the world looking at China's AI. After WAIC 2026, it is China's AI standing shoulder-to-shoulder with the world.'—from a 'governance' perspective, this defines China AI's globalization positioning: not just technology export, but governance participation.
